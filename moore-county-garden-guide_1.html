Current pest alerts — Moore County, June
Squash vine borer — peak flight period
High alert · Moths active Jun–Jul in NC Sandhills. Lay eggs at base of squash/zucchini stems. Wrap stems with kaolin clay or row covers.
Japanese Beetle — active now
High alert · Emerge Jun–Aug. Attack beans, corn, squash, okra, roses. Hand-pick into soapy water in the morning when they are sluggish.
Tomato Hornworm — watch foliage
Moderate · Large green caterpillars. Check undersides of tomato leaves daily. Look for dark droppings on leaves as first sign.
Striped & Spotted Cucumber Beetle
Moderate · Transmits bacterial wilt, which kills cucumbers within days. Use yellow sticky traps to monitor; use row covers on young plants.
Aphids — monitor okra & peppers
Low–moderate · Knock off with strong water spray. Attract ladybugs by planting dill, fennel, or yarrow nearby.
Brown Stink Bug — sweet corn & tomatoes
Low · Increasing presence in Sandhills in summer. Kaolin clay on fruit provides physical deterrent.

Organic pest & disease control — Moore County

Insect controls

  • Neem oil (Azadirachtin) — broad-spectrum; spray leaves top and bottom at dusk. Repeat every 7–14 days and after rain.
  • Insecticidal soap — kills aphids, mites, whiteflies, soft-bodied insects on contact. Do not spray in full sun or on stressed plants.
  • Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) — targets caterpillars and hornworms only. Apply to leaves actively being eaten. Rain-fast formulas last longer.
  • Spinosad — effective against Colorado potato beetles, thrips, leafminers, fire ants. OMRI-listed organic. Very low toxicity to mammals.
  • Kaolin clay — fine white powder coats leaves and stems, acting as a physical deterrent to cucumber beetles, squash vine borers, and stink bugs.
  • Row covers (spunbonded floating fabric) — physical barrier; most reliable protection against cucumber beetles, squash vine borers, aphids on young transplants.
  • Trap crops — plant Blue Hubbard squash at garden perimeter to pull squash vine borers away from your main squash crop.
  • Yellow sticky traps — monitor aphid and whitefly populations to catch infestations early.

Fungal & bacterial disease

  • Copper fungicide (organic) — effective against bacterial wilt, early blight, downy mildew, anthracnose. Apply preventively in humid NC summers.
  • Baking soda spray: 1 tsp baking soda + 1 qt water + a few drops dish soap — suppresses powdery mildew on cucurbits and squash.
  • Remove and bag infected leaves immediately. Never compost diseased plant material — it spreads disease.
  • Water at soil level only — drip irrigation or soaker hoses. Overhead watering spreads fungal spores rapidly in humid NC summers.
  • Rotate crops every year: do not plant tomatoes, peppers, or eggplant in the same bed more than once every 3 years.
  • Plant disease-resistant varieties whenever possible — look for V, F, N, T resistance codes on seed packets (NC State Extension guidance).
Wildlife in Moore County

White-tailed deer are common throughout the Sandhills. Moore County Master Gardeners note that no plant is truly deer-resistant — a hungry deer will eat anything. Deer leave ragged, torn browse marks; rabbits leave clean diagonal cuts close to the ground. Groundhogs are burrowers common in transitional woodland areas of Moore County.

Do NOT use mothballs to deter wildlife — this is illegal under federal pesticide law and dangerous to children and pets (NC State Extension, Wildlife Handbook ch. 20).

🦌 Deer prevention
  • 8-foot fence — the most reliable barrier. Deer can jump 6 feet but rarely risk a blind landing over an 8-foot obstacle. Woven wire or polypropylene deer netting both work.
  • Double fence — two 4-foot fences placed 3–4 feet apart. Deer are reluctant to jump into a narrow enclosed space.
  • Electric fence with peanut butter bait — deer touch the wire, get a deterring shock, and learn to avoid the area. Most effective when trained early in the season.
  • Putrescent egg solid sprays (Deer Off, Liquid Fence) — reapply every 2 weeks and after every rainfall. Most effective repellent category.
  • Homemade egg spray: 1 egg + 1 cup milk + 1 tbsp dish soap + 1 tbsp cooking oil per gallon of water. Spray foliage. Reapply every 2 weeks and after rain.
  • Clover perimeter planting — plant white clover around the garden. Deer graze it preferentially and leave vegetables alone (reported effective by Moore County gardeners and Carolina Country).
  • Deer dislike bitter, hairy, or thorny foliage — interplant lavender, rosemary, sage, and lamb's ear around the garden border as a deterrent.
  • Motion-activated sprinklers (e.g., Scarecrow) — startle deer at night when they are most active. Effective for 1–2 seasons before deer habituate.
  • Contact Moore County Cooperative Extension at 910-947-3188 for depredation permit information if deer damage is severe.
🐇 Rabbit prevention
  • 2-foot chicken wire fence buried 6 inches underground, bent 90° outward — rabbits dig under flat-bottomed fences. The L-shaped underground barrier stops them.
  • Rabbits leave clean, diagonal cuts at ground level — identify damage early before a colony establishes nearby.
  • Cayenne pepper spray: 2 tbsp cayenne + 1 tbsp dish soap per gallon of water. Spray around plant bases. Reapply after rain.
  • Hardware cloth cylinders around individual transplants until they are at least 12 inches tall and established.
  • Eliminate brushy hiding spots — wood piles, brush piles, and dense shrubs near the garden give rabbits the cover they need to feel safe foraging.
  • Dried blood meal sprinkled around the garden border repels rabbits and also adds nitrogen to sandy Sandhills soil — a double benefit.
🐀 Groundhog & rodent prevention
  • Bury wire mesh 12 inches deep, bent 90° outward underground. Groundhogs are powerful diggers and will burrow under standard fences.
  • The top 12 inches of fence should be unsupported and floppy — groundhogs cannot climb past a rolling, unstable top section.
  • Live trap and relocate at least 5 miles away — contact Moore County Animal Control for guidance.
  • Remove fallen fruit, overripe vegetables, bird feeders, and compost bins located near the garden — these attract all rodents and groundhogs.
  • Voles (small underground tunnelers) — wrap root vegetables, bulbs, and transplant roots in hardware cloth cylinders buried in the soil.
  • Castor oil granules around the garden perimeter repel groundhogs and moles without harming them or the soil.
  • Plant spurge (Euphorbia) around the perimeter — voles and groundhogs dislike its milky latex sap.
Sandhills sandy soil fact: Moore County's deep, well-drained sandy soils — the same type studied at the nearby NC State Sandhills Research Station in Montgomery County — drain extremely fast, losing both water and nutrients quickly. This is the defining growing challenge for Sandhills gardeners. The good news: sandy soils warm up early, are easy to work, and respond dramatically to organic matter amendments. Get a soil test before adding anything — Moore County Cooperative Extension will send your sample to NCDA&CS labs for a low-cost analysis. Call 910-947-3188.
Amending Sandhills sandy soil
  • Compost is the foundation — add 3–4 inches of finished compost and till to 8–10 inches every season. The single most effective and cost-efficient amendment for sandy soil (NC State Extension, Pender County — soils nearly identical to Moore County).
  • Cover crops — plant in fall: crimson clover, hairy vetch, or winter rye. Till in at flowering. Summer cover crops: cowpeas, buckwheat, pearl millet. These add organic matter and nitrogen simultaneously at very low cost.
  • Biochar — mixed into sandy soil at 5–10% by volume, biochar dramatically improves water and nutrient retention long-term. Can be made from clean wood scraps in a retort or purchased.
  • Aged wood chip mulch — 3–4 inch layer around plants conserves moisture, reduces watering frequency by up to 50%, and slowly feeds soil microbes as it breaks down. Use aged chips, not fresh, to avoid nitrogen tie-up.
  • Worm castings — add ¼–½ cup per transplant hole. Castings improve water-holding capacity, feed beneficial microbes, and provide slow-release nutrients.
  • Raised beds are highly recommended for Sandhills gardeners. Fill with: 60% good topsoil + 30% compost + 10% coarse vermiculite or perlite.
  • Target soil pH 6.0–6.8 for most vegetables. Sandy Sandhills soils are often slightly acidic — add agricultural lime per your soil test results to raise pH.
  • Increase fertilizer application frequency by ~20% compared to package directions for sandy soils, as nutrients leach out faster (NC State Extension).
Making organic fertilizer at home

Compost tea (liquid fertilizer)

  • Fill a 5-gallon bucket ⅓ with finished compost. Fill to top with water, add 1 tbsp unsulfured molasses (feeds microbes).
  • Aerate with a small aquarium pump for 24–36 hours to brew aerobic compost tea.
  • Strain through burlap or cheesecloth. Dilute 1:10 with water before applying as a soil drench.
  • Apply at the root zone every 2–3 weeks during the growing season. Especially valuable in leaching sandy soil.

Worm bin (vermicompost)

  • Use red wiggler worms (Eisenia fetida) in a ventilated bin with bedding of shredded cardboard or newspaper.
  • Feed kitchen scraps: vegetable peelings, coffee grounds, crushed eggshells, tea bags. Avoid meat, dairy, or oily foods.
  • Harvest dark, crumbly castings every 3–4 months. The highest-quality organic fertilizer you can make at home.
  • Apply castings as a top dressing around plants, or brew into compost tea (see above).

Organic granular blends — buy or mix your own

  • Blood meal (12-0-0) — fast nitrogen release. Use for leafy greens, corn. Also repels rabbits when sprinkled around beds.
  • Bone meal (3-15-0) — slow phosphorus for root development and transplant establishment.
  • Kelp meal (1-0-2) — micronutrients + potassium. Especially valuable in leached sandy soils that are deficient in trace minerals.
  • Greensand — very slow potassium + micronutrients. Also improves the texture and water-holding capacity of sandy soils over time.
  • Fish emulsion (5-2-2) — fast all-purpose liquid fertilizer. Apply diluted every 2–3 weeks to growing crops.
  • DIY complete blend: mix 4 parts compost + 1 part blood meal + 1 part bone meal + ½ part kelp meal. Apply 2–3 cups per 10 sq ft, worked into the top 4 inches.
